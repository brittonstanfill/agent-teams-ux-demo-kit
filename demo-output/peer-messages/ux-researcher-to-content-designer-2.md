Sharing the user needs that I think should anchor your voice and copy decisions on this flow. Flagging upfront: these are research-grounded needs, not copy prescriptions. Lines are yours to write — but I want to make sure they earn their place.

## The emotional arc to design *against*
The user is on a likely path of: confusion → suspicion → resignation. [inferred from disruption-research patterns — not directly observed in this product]

The redesign needs to move them along: **orientation → understanding → agency → closure.**

Every screen's voice job maps to one of those four:
- **Orientation** (SMS, Screen 1): name the event in plain language; signal that there's a path; reassure that support exists.
- **Understanding** (Recovery Options): explain consequences, not just labels. What does each choice mean for *me, tomorrow*?
- **Agency** (Flight selection, support): make it clear that the user is choosing, not being steered. Avoid airline-flattering language ("we are sorry for the inconvenience" reads as boilerplate at 10:46 p.m.).
- **Closure** (Confirmation): summarize what's true now, and what to do if it changes again.

## Specific voice asks, with the user need behind each

### 1. Name the event plainly
- **Need:** Users need to know what actually happened. [observed from brief]
- **Current:** "Schedule irregularity on NS482." / "Your itinerary has changed." / "NS482 DEN to LGA is no longer operating."
- **Voice ask:** Use the word "canceled" if it's canceled. Euphemism reads as evasion under stress. [recommendation]
- **What I am NOT claiming:** that I have user data showing "Schedule irregularity" specifically fails comprehension for this audience. That's an assumption [A2 in my report] worth a 5-person comprehension check if we want to be sure.

### 2. Make consequences legible, not just labels
- **Need:** Users need to choose with confidence they didn't miss a better option. [inferred]
- **Voice ask:** Every option label should answer "what does this mean for me?" in a half-sentence. E.g., not "Standby" but "Standby — you may or may not get a seat." Not just "Travel credit" but "Travel credit — keep the value, fly later."
- **Tension I want to flag:** the team will be tempted to make this brief. Brevity is good, but stress users re-read; clarity beats brevity. I'd push back on copy that is shorter than it is clear.

### 3. Surface entitlements as care, not policy
- **Need:** Users need to receive what they're entitled to without hunting for it. [observed from brief: hotel/meal in collapsed FAQ]
- **Voice ask:** Frame hotel and meal support as *part of the plan*, not as policy disclosures. "Tonight's hotel is on us" reads very differently from "You may be eligible for hotel accommodation per policy." [recommendation]
- **Important constraint:** I cannot tell you what is actually offered — the brief says "do not invent legal obligations, airline policy, or compensation rules." Voice can warm up the framing of whatever the policy actually is. It cannot promise things the airline doesn't deliver. Please coordinate with whoever owns policy facts.

### 4. Reassure without being saccharine
- **Need:** Users need to trust that the airline is on their side. [inferred]
- **Voice ask:** Reassurance lines should *do something*, not just emote. "We are sorry for the inconvenience" performs no work. "We'll get you to New York. Here are your options." performs orientation + agency in one breath. [recommendation]
- **Pushback:** if anyone proposes apology copy as a reassurance strategy, I'd ask: what behavior do we expect it to change? If the answer is "users will feel better," that's a hypothesis, not a finding.

### 5. Confirmation copy should let the user stop worrying
- **Need:** Users need a durable, self-contained recap so they don't call to verify. [observed from brief]
- **Voice ask:** The confirmation needs to read like a receipt + plan, not a thank-you note. "Your changes have been applied" gives the user nothing to hold onto. Spell out: new flight, hotel, baggage, when to be where, and what to do if something else changes. [recommendation]

## Things I'd like you to push back on me about
- Whether the "orientation → understanding → agency → closure" arc is real or just nice-sounding. It's [inferred]. If you have content-research evidence that disruption users actually want something different (e.g., they want apology *first*), I want to hear it before we lock voice.
- Whether plain-language framing of entitlements ("on us") risks over-promising. I'd rather we err on warmth and have legal trim it, but you and the policy owner have more context.
