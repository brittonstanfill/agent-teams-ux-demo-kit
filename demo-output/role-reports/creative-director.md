# Creative Director — Northstar Canceled-Flight Recovery

## 1. Aesthetic anchor (recommendation)

**Things 3's deliberateness × Apple Health's after-hours calm × a hotel concierge's quiet competence at 11 p.m.**

Not Linear (too engineer-cool for a scared traveler). Not Stripe (too transactional — this is not a checkout). Not airline-brand warmth (too marketing). The reference is a *single trusted adult speaking quietly in a dim room*: confident, specific, finite. The screen should feel like it already knows what to do and is walking the user through it, not asking them to choose their own adventure.

## 2. Three moves worth stealing (recommendation)

1. **Typography that declares, then explains.** Things-style — one large, plain, confident H1 ("Your 6:15 a.m. is canceled. Here's what we're doing.") followed by short, generous body text. No clever phrasing, no apology-paragraph. The hierarchy itself does the reassurance.
2. **A warm-neutral palette, not a brand palette.** Apple Health after-dark — soft off-white surface, warm charcoal text, one restrained accent (think warm amber or muted teal) for the recommended path. No airline navy, no alert-red banners. Red is for genuine danger, not for "your flight is canceled" — that's already understood by the user.
3. **Generous vertical rhythm, one decision per screen.** Density is the enemy of a tired thumb at 10:46 p.m. Big tap targets, lots of breathing room, one primary action that's *unmistakably* the primary action. The screen should feel like it's exhaling, not shouting.

## 3. Failure mode to avoid

**"Airline corporate apology page with three tabs."** Sterile blue header, "We apologize for the inconvenience," a tabbed picker that makes the user adjudicate between "New flights / Travel credit / Standby" while exhausted, hotel voucher buried in an FAQ accordion. Also avoid: Bootstrap-admin density, fintech-cold monochrome, and any "Recommended" badge that doesn't say *why*.

## 4. Quality bar

- **Visual/interaction craft:** A designer scanning the first screen identifies three deliberate moves (type hierarchy, palette restraint, decision-per-screen rhythm) before reading any copy.
- **Behavioral ethics:** Six months from now, the traveler can describe what the airline did for them that night without checking their email — because the flow named the entitlement out loud, not in a footnote.
- **Accessibility:** A screen-reader user on the SMS link reaches "trip confirmed, hotel booked, here's your next step" without a phone call, and the reading order matches the visual hierarchy on every screen.

## 5. Pushback sentences for the run

1. "That's the consensus-safe answer — what's the version you'd put in your portfolio?"
2. "This reads like an apology page. The user already knows we're sorry. What does the *competent* version say?"
3. "We're letting the user adjudicate a choice we should have already made for them. Where's the recommended path, and why is it recommended?"

## Sign-off on visual authoring

**Call: signoff-with-nits.**

The three borderline calls all earn the anchor. (a) **Serif H1, signed off.** 30px / weight 500 / -0.01em on Iowan Old Style is restrained, not editorial — at this size it reads as *deliberate*, the slowed beat the brief asked for. Inter would have read efficient; this reads attended-to. (b) **Equal-width secondary, signed off.** The held-card's tinted moss surface plus the eyebrow pulse carry the path; the secondary is a peer in *width* but not in *weight*. Hierarchy via fill, not via button-size — that's the move. (c) **Honest-disabled bag card, signed off.** Showing the system noticed is the concierge tell. Hiding it would have read efficient-but-cold.

**Nits (appendix, do not revise):**
- Eyebrow "Flight NS482 — tomorrow" leans operational; "Tomorrow morning · NS 482" would feel more concierge. Stet for now.
- Screen 2 chip "Take credit instead" is doing escape-hatch work in the filter row — fine as authored, watch in research.
