It is 10:46 p.m. Your 6:15 a.m. flight just disappeared. Build the screens that meet the moment.

A redesigned recovery flow for a tired, mobile-only traveler who needs to be somewhere tomorrow. Stop hiding what they're owed. Make the next physical action — pick a flight, claim a hotel, talk to a person — the most visible thing on every screen.

Guardrails

Accessibility — built in, not bolted on

  • Hit targets 48px or larger. One-thumb operation at 10:46 p.m. is the design target (WCAG 2.5.5 Target Size, AAA-aimed).
  • Body text ≥15px; titles in a serif with 1.12 line-height for readability under fatigue (WCAG 1.4.4 Resize Text).
  • Color is never the only signal. "Needs you" pairs amber with an icon and the literal word; "active" pairs green with a checkmark (WCAG 1.4.1 Use of Color).
  • Screen-reader-first prose. Every flight card has a single labeled name; layovers and entitlement consequences are read aloud, not implied by layout (WCAG 1.3.1 Info and Relationships).
  • prefers-reduced-motion respected. No animated state transitions in the disruption flow.
  • Low-bandwidth path. The packet screen is plain HTML and works offline; SMS thread is the always-available fallback channel.

Trust — the line between nudge and pressure

  • Refund and travel credit are a peer to rebook, not a buried "other policies" link.
  • Hotel and meal entitlements load on the first screen so the user doesn't pay out of pocket while figuring the flow out.
  • "Talk to a person" is on every screen. The business goal is fewer unnecessary calls — not fewer necessary ones.
  • No countdown timers, no fake scarcity. Wait times and seat availability are dynamic but never used to coerce a faster click.
  • Consequences are stated before commitment, not surprised at confirmation. Hotel/meal status appears on every path card.
  • One contact handle, not a maze. "Reply CHANGE to this thread" keeps the user with the same agent and trip context.
